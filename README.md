# lunar-solar-eclipses-ramadan-1551-2649
Computational analysis of lunar and solar eclipse occurrences during Ramadan (1551–2649 CE) using SQL and tabular Islamic calendar modeling.



Abstract
This study evaluates the historical recurrence of a specific astronomical configuration: the occurrence of a lunar eclipse during the first half of Ramadan and a solar eclipse during the second half of the same Ramadan. Using tabular Islamic calendar computations, Ramadan date ranges were generated for the Gregorian years 1551–2649 CE. These were cross-referenced with recorded lunar and solar eclipse datasets using SQL-based temporal range joins. The analysis demonstrates that this eclipse configuration occurs more than once within the examined interval. Therefore, under a literal astronomical interpretation, the configuration is not historically unique within the tested timeframe. This study does not assess theological interpretation or textual authenticity, but focuses solely on astronomical recurrence.


Introduction
Certain Islamic narrations describe a sequence of astronomical events associated with eschatological expectations, including the occurrence of both a lunar and a solar eclipse during the month of Ramadan. In contemporary discussions, this configuration is sometimes described as unique or historically unprecedented.
The present study does not evaluate theological interpretation, textual authenticity, or doctrinal implications. Instead, it addresses a strictly astronomical question: whether the described eclipse configuration is historically unique within the limits of reliable astronomical computation.
To evaluate this, Ramadan date ranges were generated using a tabular Islamic calendar model, and cross-referenced with established solar and lunar eclipse datasets. The analysis was restricted to the Gregorian years 1551–2649 CE. This interval corresponds to the stable range supported by modern high-precision ephemeris models used in eclipse prediction. Within this window, eclipse timing and classification are computationally robust and suitable for historical recurrence analysis.
The motivation for this study arises from the repeated contemporary assertion that such an eclipse configuration is singular or without precedent. Given the availability of precise astronomical data and computational tools, the claim can be evaluated empirically. Accordingly, this study tests whether:
    Lunar eclipses occur within Ramadan.
    Solar eclipses occur within Ramadan.
    A lunar eclipse in the first half of Ramadan and a solar eclipse in the second half of the same Ramadan constitute a unique historical event within the analyzed interval.
    The study is limited to astronomical frequency and temporal coincidence. It does not address interpretive, theological, or doctrinal dimensions beyond the scope of computational
    astronomy.
